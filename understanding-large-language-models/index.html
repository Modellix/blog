The Transformer Architecture

At the heart of every modern LLM is the transformer, introduced in the 2017 paper Attention Is All You Need. The key innovation is the self-attention mechanism, which allows the model to weigh relationships between all tokens in a sequence simultaneously rather than processing them one by one.

This simple operation, repeated across many layers and attention heads, gives the model its remarkable ability to capture context.

Training at Scale

Training an LLM involves two major phases:

  1. Pre-training — The model learns language patterns from a massive corpus of text using next-token prediction.
  2. Fine-tuning — The model is refined on curated datasets, often with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF).

The compute cost is staggering. A frontier model can require thousands of GPUs running for months.

What LLMs Are Good At

  • Summarization and rewriting
  • Code generation and debugging
  • Translation across languages
  • Question answering over unstructured text

Current Limitations

Despite their power, LLMs still struggle with:

  • Hallucination — generating plausible but incorrect facts
  • Reasoning over long contexts — performance degrades as input length grows
  • Real-time knowledge — models only know what was in their training data

Looking Ahead

The field is moving fast. Mixture-of-experts architectures, longer context windows, and better retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipelines are all active research areas that promise to address today’s shortcomings.

Understanding how these models work is no longer optional for developers — it’s becoming a core skill.
