Seedance vs Kling: A Developer’s Comparison

If you’ve landed here, you’ve already narrowed the field to two of 2026’s strongest AI video models — Seedance from ByteDance and Kling from Kuaishou — and you want to pick one to build on. Before the details, one myth to clear out, because nearly every roundup repeats it: “Seedance is cheaper” is not a fact, it’s a rounding error. Most articles quote Seedance’s subscription rate (~$0.031 per second, a consumer plan averaged out) against Kling’s API rate and declare a winner. Those are different billing worlds, and comparing them is meaningless.

Compared like-for-like — same platform, same API metering, same day — the honest answer is more interesting: which one is cheaper depends on the tier and resolution you pick, and it can flip entirely on one question you probably haven’t asked yet — do you need audio? This guide gives you a same-platform per-second price table, the audio math that changes the answer, and a per-scenario pick. Whether you searched seedance vs kling or kling vs seedance, it’s the same decision, and it’s below.

The audio cost tipping point

Here’s the factor that flips the whole comparison, and almost no roundup does the math on it: how audio gets billed isn’t a fixed property of either model — it depends on the platform, so a price that looks lower can quietly exclude the sound you need.

The catch is that the same model bills audio differently depending on where you run it:

  • On fal (rates linked above), Kling 3.0 Pro at 1080p is $0.112/sec with audio off and $0.168/sec with audio on — roughly a 50% premium to turn sound on, before any voice-control extras. There, audio is an opt-in surcharge.
  • On an aggregator like Modellix, Kling V3’s per-second rate already includes native audio, and Seedance’s audio-capable I2V tiers do too — while Seedance’s plain text-to-video tier ships audio-off.

So the real trap isn’t “one model is dearer” — it’s comparing an audio-off rate on one platform against an audio-on rate on another and calling it a verdict. That’s exactly how the SERP’s contradictory numbers happen. Before you compare, pin down two things: does this specific tier include audio, and does my deliverable need sound at all? For silent motion footage — B-roll, dynamic backgrounds, product loops — you can pick the cheapest audio-off tier on either model and never pay for sound you won’t use. For a finished clip with dialogue or a synced score, only compare audio-included rates, or you’re pricing half a product. Ask “do I need sound in the output?” before “which is cheaper,” because the first answer decides which numbers are even comparable.

Quality and motion: what each is built for

The SERP is full of contradictory claims here — one article crowns Kling for motion, the next crowns Seedance — because they’re grading different things. Split by dimension and the picture is consistent:

Kling leans into camera and physics. Native 4K at 60 FPS and an “AI director” mode that composes up to six distinct shots — each with its own framing and camera move — inside a single 15-second clip make it strong for cinematic, movement-heavy sequences where the camera is doing the work. If you want a crane shot that resolves into a tracking shot, Kling’s structured multi-shot control is built for that.

Seedance leans into performance and identity. Its edge is character consistency across a scene plus audio-driven action — motion coordinated with the sound it generates in the same pass. (Multilingual lip-sync is reported by third parties but isn’t itemized in ByteDance’s official 2.0 notes, so treat it as likely-but-unconfirmed rather than a guarantee.) For a talking character, a narrated sequence, or anything where the subject (not the camera) carries the shot, that audio-motion integration matters more than raw camera athletics.

Neither is “better” in the abstract. Kling is the stronger camera; Seedance is the stronger performer. Which you want depends on what’s moving in your shot.

How to choose

Skip the “it depends” cop-out — here’s the decision, by what you’re actually making:

  • Choose Seedance if your output has sound: dialogue, narration, a talking-character performance, or a synced score. Audio is bundled, character identity holds across the scene, and the rich multi-input prompt shines when you’re conditioning on reference assets. It’s also the lower entry price for basic text-to-video.
  • Choose Kling if you’re producing camera-driven footage at scale: cinematic B-roll, dynamic backgrounds, product motion. You get native 4K at 60 FPS and a six-shot AI director that handles complex camera sequences in one clip — and if the work is silent, you can run an audio-off tier and not pay for sound you won’t use.
  • Still unsure? Run both on your actual prompt through one API key, compare the two outputs and the two costs directly, and let the result decide. For a close call like this one, an hour of A/B beats a week of reading comparisons.

FAQ

Is Seedance cheaper than Kling?
Not as a blanket rule. On a same-platform basis, Seedance’s entry text-to-video tier starts a bit lower (~$0.08 vs ~$0.10/sec), but its premium and 4K tiers climb much higher than Kling’s ceiling. Audio muddies it further: whether a rate includes sound or charges it as a surcharge depends on the platform, so a clip with audio can rank differently than the base rates suggest. Compare the specific tier, resolution, and audio handling on the platform you’ll actually use.

Seedance or Kling — which is better for video?
For sound-driven work (dialogue, narration, a synced score), Seedance. For silent, camera-driven, high-volume footage in native 4K, Kling. Both cap at 15-second multi-shot clips, so the deciding factors are audio needs and whether the camera or the subject carries your shot.

What’s the difference between Kling 3.0 and Seedance 2.0?
They’re the current flagships of two rivals, both released early 2026. Kling 3.0 emphasizes a 60 FPS native-4K “AI director” with up to six camera shots per clip; Seedance 2.0 emphasizes bundled two-channel audio and a mixed-modal prompt that accepts up to nine images plus video and audio references.

Is Kling a Chinese company?
Kling is made by Kuaishou, and Seedance by ByteDance — both Chinese firms. If data residency or compliance matters for your use case, evaluate each vendor’s terms directly; accessing either through an aggregation layer doesn’t change whose model is running.
